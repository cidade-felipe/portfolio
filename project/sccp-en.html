Student Course Completion Prediction

Analysis and prediction of completion in online courses, with a preparation pipeline, engagement exploration and supervised models to predict completion.

1. Introduction

Online education grew significantly, especially during the COVID-19 pandemic. In this context, educational institutions had to adapt and implement new technologies, which led to stronger technology integration in teaching. In addition, online education made course access easier for students in remote areas, contributing to democratization of access to education.

This work aims to perform exploratory data analysis to understand student and course characteristics, identify factors that influence course completion through engagement patterns, and generate actionable insights for follow-up.

2. Objectives

The general objective of this work is to analyze factors that lead students to complete or not complete online courses.

The specific objectives of this work are:

  • Perform exploratory data analysis to understand student and course characteristics.
  • Identify factors that influence course completion through engagement patterns.
  • Generate actionable insights for follow-up.

3. Data source and structure

Data comes from a dataset available on Kaggle, available here. The dataset contains student information, such as age, gender, time spent on the platform, educational background, among others, and course information, such as course name, duration, price, and others. The target variable is in the "Completed" column, indicating whether the student completed the course.

4. Data extraction and preparation

The dataset was extracted from Kaggle and saved in the file "student_course_completion.csv". The file was loaded with the pandas library in Python, and data was explored to understand student and course characteristics. The source code is available here.

5. Visual analysis and insights

Exploratory data analyses were performed to understand factors that influence course completion through engagement patterns.

Missed assignments vs completion

Students who completed the course have a lower median of missed assignments and a more concentrated distribution. Those who did not complete show greater dispersion and higher maximum values. This suggests that missing assignments is not an isolated event, but a recurring behavior associated with dropout. A student does not drop out because they missed one assignment, but due to progressive disengagement caused by a sequence of missed tasks.

Days since last login

Students who completed the course tend to have fewer days since last login, with most values concentrated near zero. Students who did not complete show long tails, with many cases of long periods without access. The longer the time without logging in, the lower the chance of completion.

Total time dedicated to the course

Students who completed the course show a clearly higher median of dedicated hours, with distribution shifted toward higher values. Students who did not complete concentrate on fewer hours, with some outliers indicating occasional attempts but no consistency.

Here we see the difference between continuous effort and sporadic effort. Dedicating more time does not guarantee completion, but dedicating little almost guarantees the opposite.

Video completion rate

The distribution of students who completed is clearly shifted toward higher video consumption rates, while students who did not complete concentrate in medium and lower values. This indicates that watching content through to the end is a strong proxy for real engagement. It is not just accessing the platform, but consuming content consistently.

Effort versus performance

There is a general trend where more dedicated hours are associated with better quiz averages, but the most important aspect is visual group separation. Students who complete appear more often in the higher-effort and better-performance region, while those who do not complete are more spread across low and intermediate levels.

This chart is great for discussion because it shows that effort helps, but does not explain everything. Some students study a lot and still do not complete, opening room for factors such as organization, conceptual difficulties or personal context.

Correlation between variables

Correlations with the Completed variable are moderate, not exaggerated, which is expected in real behavioral data. Positive correlation stands out with Video_Completion_Rate and Assignments_Submitted, and negative correlation with Assignments_Missed. The strongest highlight in the map is the almost perfect negative correlation between Assignments_Submitted and Assignments_Missed, which is coherent and helps validate dataset consistency.

6. Conclusion

Exploratory data analysis revealed that factors influencing course completion are multivariate, combining engagement, effort and academic performance. No single variable fully explains completion. Therefore, completion in online courses can be understood as a multivariate phenomenon, combining engagement, effort and academic performance.

7. Next steps

Natural next steps would be publishing visuals as a web dashboard, creating a ready-to-consume data layer, and adding more detailed regional comparisons.
